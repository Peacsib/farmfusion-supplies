Professional Feed Guides

Comprehensive feeding guidelines based on industry best practices. For personalized recommendations tailored to your flock or herd, WhatsApp FarmFusion.

Broiler Feed Program (0-42 Days)

Broilers require a three-phase feeding program for optimal growth and feed conversion ratio (FCR target: 1.55-1.70 by day 42).

Phase 1: Starter (Days 0-10)

  • Broiler Starter Crumbs – 22-24% protein, high energy (2,950-3,150 kcal/kg ME)
  • Feed per bird: ~450g total over 10 days
  • Key nutrients: Lysine, methionine for early muscle development
  • Management: Ensure all chicks have feed in crops within 24 hours of placement

Phase 2: Grower (Days 11-24)

  • Broiler Grower Crumbs/Pellets – 18-21% protein, sustained energy
  • Feed per bird: ~1.2kg total over grower phase
  • Purpose: Continued muscle growth and skeletal development
  • Options: Grower Concentrate (mix with maize for cost savings)

Phase 3: Finisher (Days 25-42)

  • Broiler Finisher Pellets – 16-18% protein, balanced for fat deposition
  • Feed per bird: ~2.5kg total to market weight
  • Value options: Broiler Value StarGrow & Grow-Fin for cost-effective finishing
  • Total feed to market: Approximately 4kg per bird

💡 Tip: Provide clean, fresh water at all times. Water intake is 1.5-2x feed intake by weight.

Layer Feed Program (Day-Old to Peak Production)

Layer chickens require precise nutrition for optimal egg production, strong eggshells, and sustained laying capacity.

Chick Starter Phase (0-8 Weeks)

  • Chick Starter Mash – High protein for rapid early growth
  • Feed per chick: ~2kg total over 8 weeks
  • Management: Provide vitamins/electrolytes during vaccinations to prevent growth loss
  • Target: Meet or exceed breed growth curve by 10%

Grower Phase (9-17 Weeks)

  • Growers Mash – Balanced nutrition for skeletal and organ development
  • Feed per pullet: ~5kg total over grower phase
  • Focus: Body weight management and flock uniformity
  • Critical: Avoid stress; maintain consistent feeding schedule

Pre-Lay/Developer (18-20 Weeks)

  • Layer Developer / Pre-Lay Mash – Transition diet before laying
  • Purpose: Prepare reproductive system for egg production
  • Key: Gradual transition to prevent digestive upset

Production Phase (21+ Weeks)

  • Layers Mash Phase 1 & 2 – 16-18% protein, 3.5%+ calcium for strong shells
  • Feed per hen: ~110-120g per day during peak production
  • Essential nutrients: Methionine, lysine for egg weight; calcium carbonate for shells
  • Layers Concentrate: Mix with maize for economical feeding

💡 Tip: Inadequate calcium leads to weak shells. Ensure constant access to clean water—dehydration reduces egg production immediately.

Road Runner Feed (Indigenous/Free-Range Poultry)

Road Runner chickens are Zimbabwe's indigenous breed, popular for their superior taste and adaptability to free-range systems. They thrive on natural foods supplemented with quality feed.

Natural Diet Foundation

  • Scavenging foods: Sorghum, rapoko (finger millet), maize, green vegetables
  • Protein sources: Insects, lizards, worms (natural foraging)
  • Benefits: Tougher, tastier meat; healthier birds; lower feed costs
  • Growing demand: Popular with restaurants across Zimbabwe

Supplemental Feed Program

  • RR1 Starter – Energy and protein-rich for young chicks (day-old to 8 weeks)
  • Feeding: Easy-to-digest feeds; provide variety for mineral/vitamin balance
  • RR2 Grower/Finisher – Supports growth in free-range conditions (9 weeks to market)
  • RR Breeder/Layer – For breeding flocks and laying hens

Feeding Guidelines

  • Amount: Approximately ½ cup (100-120g) per bird per day when supplementing
  • Water: Must be available at all times
  • Sunlight: Regular access essential for vitamin D and health
  • Growth rate: Slower than broilers but superior meat quality

💡 Tip: Road Runners are excellent for smallholder farmers. Start with 1 rooster + 2 hens; expect 60+ birds within one year with proper management.

Pig Feed Program (Birth to Market)

Pigs require age-appropriate nutrition at each growth stage. A growing pig eats approximately 4% of its body weight daily.

Creep Phase (~1-4 Weeks)

  • Pig Creep Meal – Highly digestible, rich in protein and essential amino acids
  • Feed per piglet: ~10kg total during creep phase
  • Purpose: Support rapid growth, develop immune system, minimize post-weaning stress
  • Critical: Feed quality and palatability paramount for developing digestive systems

Weaner Phase (5kg-35kg Body Weight)

  • Pig Weaner Meal – 10-20% protein, 3,000-3,200 kcal/kg energy
  • Feed per weaner: ~26kg total to reach 35kg body weight
  • Daily intake: A 50-pound pig needs roughly 2 pounds (0.9kg) of feed
  • Management: Ensure smooth transition from creep to weaner feed

Grower Phase (35kg-60kg)

  • Pig Grower Meal – 16% crude protein, 3,200 kcal DE
  • Grower Concentrate – Mix 2 parts concentrate + 3 parts crushed maize by weight
  • Purpose: Efficient weight gain and muscle development
  • Calcium:Phosphorus ratio: Maintain 1:1 to 1.5:1 for bone health

Finisher Phase (60kg to Market)

  • Grower/Finisher Meal – Day 57 to slaughter (typically 147 days)
  • Feed per pig: ~225kg total by market weight
  • Daily intake: A 200-pound pig needs about 8 pounds (3.6kg) daily
  • Finisher Concentrate: Cost-effective option mixed with maize

Breeding Stock

  • Lactating Sow Meal – Base 2kg/day + 0.5kg per live piglet; ~3kg/day pre-farrowing
  • Pig Boar & Sow – Maintenance ration for breeding stock (1-2kg per animal per day)
  • Supplements: Provide legume hay (groundnut haulms, bean pods, cowpea residues)

💡 Tip: Zinc, copper, iron, manganese, and selenium support immunity and hoof health. Organic forms absorb better than inorganic minerals.

Rabbit Feed Program (All Life Stages)

Rabbits require high-fiber diets with precise nutrient balance. A well-fed rabbit produces quality meat and maintains optimal health.

Essential Nutritional Requirements

  • Fiber: Minimum 14% (critical for digestive health and peristaltic action)
  • Protein: Maximum 20% (excess can cause health issues)
  • Fat: Maximum 5% (prevents obesity)
  • Calcium: Maximum 1.1% (prevents urinary problems)

Young Rabbits (0-6 Months)

  • Rabbit Starter – Alfalfa-based pellets, high in protein and calcium
  • Purpose: Support rapid growth and bone development
  • Ideal for: Young rabbits, pregnant does, nursing mothers
  • Feeding: Unlimited pellets plus fresh hay

Adult Rabbits (6+ Months)

  • Rabbit Pellets – Timothy hay-based, lower protein and calcium
  • Daily amount: ¼ cup per 5 lbs body weight (high-fiber pellets)
  • Hay: Unlimited high-quality grass hay (primary diet component)
  • Greens: 1 cup fresh leafy greens per 2 lbs body weight

Grower Rabbits (Market Production)

  • Rabbit Grower – Balanced nutrition for efficient weight gain
  • Feed intake: Approximately 2-4% of body weight daily
  • Key nutrients: Methionine, lysine for muscle development
  • Benefits: Improved digestion, reduced obesity risk

Important Feeding Notes

  • Water: Must be available at all times (critical for health)
  • Avoid: Pellets with whole nuts, seeds, fruits (muesli-style)
  • First ingredient: Should always be grass or hay
  • Fiber source: Lignocellulose moves rapidly through colon as hard fecal pellets

💡 Tip: Rabbits practice cecotrophy (eating soft fecal pellets). This is normal and essential for nutrient absorption—don't prevent it!

Goat Feed Program (All Life Stages)

Goats are ruminants with four-chambered stomachs. They are primarily browsers (prefer leaves and bushes) but also graze on grass.

Core Nutritional Requirements

  • Six essential nutrients: Proteins, carbohydrates, fats, vitamins, minerals, water
  • Forage foundation: 70% of diet should be quality hay, browse, grasses, legumes
  • Daily intake: 2-4 pounds of hay per 100 pounds body weight
  • Water: Constant access essential (goats are sensitive to dehydration)

Maintenance Feeding (Non-Productive)

  • Goat Feed - Maintenance – Minimum feed for non-growing, non-pregnant, non-lactating goats
  • Amount: 1-2kg per animal per day
  • Purpose: Maintain body weight and warmth
  • Supplements: Legume hay (groundnut haulms, bean pods, cowpea residues)

Growing Goats (Kids to Market)

  • Goat Feed - Nuts – Concentrated nutrition for growth
  • Protein: Essential for muscle growth and development
  • Energy: Carbohydrates provide fuel for daily activities
  • Browse: Encourage natural browsing behavior for balanced nutrition

Lactating Does (Milk Production)

  • Daily intake: 3-5% of body weight in dry matter
  • Example: 50kg doe producing milk needs up to 2.5kg dry feed daily
  • Protein boost: Support milk production with quality protein sources
  • Minerals: Free-choice loose minerals essential for lactation

Forage & Browse Options

  • Preferred: Leaves from trees and bushes (natural browsing)
  • Grasses: Various grass types for grazing
  • Legumes: High-protein options like groundnut haulms
  • Crop residues: Bean pods, cowpea residues, maize stover

Year-Round Forage Planning

  • Critical: Develop annual forage program for consistent feed availability
  • Dry season: Store hay and crop residues
  • Wet season: Maximize fresh browse and pasture
  • Minerals: Provide free-choice minerals year-round

💡 Tip: Goats regurgitate and chew cud to break down cellulose. Ensure adequate fiber for proper rumen function and digestive health.
